01 — Epidemiology

The Most Common Benign Pelvic Tumour

Uterine fibroids (leiomyomas) are benign monoclonal tumours arising from uterine smooth muscle cells (myometrium). Their true prevalence is underestimated due to the high rate of asymptomatic cases.

Indian Prevalence Data click to expand

Indian studies report a prevalence of 24–30% in reproductive-age women (Gupta et al., JOGI). Peak incidence 30–45 years. High prevalence of vitamin D deficiency in Indian women is a contributory factor. Iron deficiency anaemia at baseline (NFHS-5: 57% of women aged 15–49) compounds fibroid-related menorrhagia, making earlier intervention more critical.

02 — Pathophysiology

Hormonal & Genetic Drivers

Fibroids are monoclonal in origin — each tumour arises from a single myometrial smooth muscle cell. Their growth is primarily driven by oestrogen and progesterone, with critical roles for somatic genetic mutations and extracellular matrix dysregulation.

Hormonal Dependency — Oestrogen, Progesterone & Vasculature click to expand

Oestrogen

  • ↑ ERα and ERβ expression in fibroid tissue vs normal myometrium
  • Promotes growth factor (IGF-1, EGF) secretion
  • Explains absence before menarche and regression post-menopause

Progesterone

  • Acts synergistically with oestrogen — critical for growth (not oestrogen alone)
  • Promotes cell proliferation and inhibits apoptosis
  • Target of SPRMs (selective progesterone receptor modulators)

Vasculature

  • Fibroids have abnormal vasculature with aberrant arteries
  • Submucosal type disrupts spiral arteriole remodelling → AUB
  • VEGF over-expression → basis of UAE treatment
Genetic Mutations — MED12, HMGA2, FH, COL4A5/6 click to expand
MED12
~70%

Most frequent somatic mutation; exon 2 hotspot. Mediator complex subunit — regulates transcription. Loss of function = dysregulated cell growth.

HMGA2
~20%

High mobility group AT-hook 2. Rearrangements at 12q14-15. Promotes IGF2BP2 — associated with larger fibroids.

FH
Rare

Fumarate hydratase (Krebs cycle). Germline mutation → HLRCC — hereditary leiomyomatosis & renal cell carcinoma. High-yield: aggressive fibroids in young women.

COL4A5/6
~4%

Collagen type IV deletion at Xq22.3. Associated with large exophytic fibroids. ECM-driven pathogenesis.

Gross & Microscopic Pathology + Degeneration Types click to expand

Macroscopic

  • Well-circumscribed, pseudo-encapsulated (not a true capsule)
  • Firm, rubbery texture; whorled pale cut surface
  • Size: mm to >30 cm (record: 45 kg removed)
  • Solitary or multiple (70% of cases); average 6–7 fibroids if multiple

Microscopic

  • Whorled bundles of smooth muscle cells — uniform, cigar-shaped nuclei
  • Abundant collagen ECM between muscle bundles
  • No mitoses (<5 mitoses/10 HPF), no nuclear atypia (contrast with leiomyosarcoma)
  • Variants: cellular, bizarre (symplastic), mitotically active — all benign

Degeneration types (when fibroid outgrows blood supply): Hyaline (most common, 65%) → Cystic → Calcification (post-menopausal) → Red/Carneous (pregnancy — acute haemorrhagic infarction) → Myxoid

⚠ Note: Leiomyosarcoma (LMS) is a de novo malignancy — it does not arise from malignant transformation of benign fibroids. Do not confuse with degeneration.

03 — Classification

Types by Location

The anatomical location of a fibroid is the primary determinant of its clinical presentation. The same size fibroid can cause dramatically different symptoms depending on its relationship to the endometrial cavity and uterine serosa.

Most Common

Intramural (IM)

Within the myometrial wall, fully contained. ~70% of all fibroids.

Most Symptomatic

Submucosal (SM)

Distorts endometrial cavity. FIGO Types 0–3. Critical for AUB and infertility.

Bulk Symptoms

Subserosal (SS)

Projects from uterine surface outward. Causes pelvic pressure, urinary frequency.

Torsion Risk

Pedunculated

Attached by a stalk — can be intracavitary (SM) or extracavitary (SS). Risk of torsion.

FIGO Type 8

Cervical / Parasitic

In cervical stroma or attached to other pelvic organs. Rare. FIGO Type 8 — "Other".

Intramural
Intramural Fibroid
Entirely within the myometrium, not distorting the endometrial cavity or serosal surface. The most common type (~70%). May enlarge significantly before becoming symptomatic.
  • Menorrhagia if large enough to disrupt endometrial vascular remodelling
  • Pelvic heaviness and dysmenorrhoea with large fibroids
  • Subfertility if cavity significantly distorted (FIGO Type 3)
  • Irregular, non-tender uterine enlargement on examination
  • Management: medical first-line; myomectomy (lap/open) or UAE if symptomatic

04 — FIGO Classification

Leiomyoma Subclassification

The FIGO leiomyoma subclassification system (Munro et al. 2011, updated 2018) defines 9 types (0–8) based on the fibroid's relationship to the endometrial cavity, myometrium, and serosal surface. Types 0–3 are submucosal; Types 4–6 are intramural/subserosal; Types 7–8 are special categories.

05 — Symptoms

Clinical Presentation

Symptoms depend on fibroid size, number, and most importantly, location. Up to 50% of fibroids are asymptomatic and discovered incidentally on imaging or examination.

Symptom Categories — AUB, Bulk, Pain, Reproductive click to expand
Abnormal Uterine Bleeding (AUB)
🩸
Menorrhagia (Heavy Menstrual Bleeding)

Most common symptom. Defined as ≥80 mL/cycle. Submucosal fibroids predominate — disrupt endometrial spiral arteriole remodelling, impair haemostasis (altered coagulation factors locally), and increase endometrial surface area. Can cause iron deficiency anaemia.

📅
Dysmenorrhoea

Particularly with submucosal fibroids. Uterine cramping as fibroid distorts cavity. Also: intermenstrual bleeding (polymenorrhagia) with pedunculated intracavitary types.

🔴
Post-coital Bleeding

Less common; may occur with cervical or prolapsed submucosal fibroids.

Bulk / Pressure Symptoms
💧
Urinary Symptoms

Frequency and urgency from anterior fibroid compressing bladder. Hydronephrosis (rare) from lateral compression of ureter. Urinary retention from large cervical fibroid (rare but high-yield).

🔵
Pelvic Pressure / Heaviness

Particularly with large intramural or subserosal fibroids. Feeling of "fullness" in lower abdomen.

💩
Bowel Symptoms

Constipation or tenesmus from posterior fibroid pressing on rectosigmoid.

Pain
⚡
Acute Pelvic Pain

Red degeneration (pregnancy), torsion (pedunculated), or acute haemorrhage within a fibroid. May mimic appendicitis or ovarian torsion.

😣
Dyspareunia

Deep dyspareunia from posterior intramural or broad ligament fibroids. Prolapsed pedunculated fibroid through cervix → apareunia.

Reproductive
🤰
Infertility

Submucosal fibroids (especially Types 0–2) are most clearly associated. Mechanisms: impaired sperm transport, embryo implantation failure, altered endometrial receptivity (HOXA10, HOXA11 suppression), disrupted uterine contractility.

💔
Recurrent Pregnancy Loss

Cavity-distorting fibroids. Mechanical obstruction of embryo implantation site, abnormal endometrial vascularity, elevated uterine contractility.

50% Asymptomatic — Key Incidental Finding click to expand
50%

Asymptomatic — Incidental Finding

Approximately 50% of women with fibroids have no symptoms. Discovery is often incidental during pelvic ultrasound for another indication, or on bimanual examination. Asymptomatic fibroids do not require treatment — only surveillance.

Clinical Examination Findings click to expand
  • → Enlarged uterus on abdominal examination (may be visible as suprapubic mass if large)
  • → Bimanual: enlarged, irregular, firm, non-tender uterus (tenderness suggests degeneration/torsion)
  • → Mobile uterus (adhesions suggest endometriosis or PID)
  • → Speculum: cervix may appear deviated; prolapsed pedunculated fibroid visible
  • → Signs of anaemia: pallor of conjunctivae, tachycardia
  • → Note: uterine size classically described in obstetric weeks (e.g., "16-week size uterus")

06 — Complications

Clinical Complications

Fibroids can cause significant morbidity through multiple mechanisms — haematological, reproductive, obstetric, and rarely acute surgical emergencies.

Complication Cards — Haematological, Obstetric, Acute, Fertility, Surgical, Malignancy click to expand
🩸
Haematological
  • Iron deficiency anaemia — chronic blood loss from menorrhagia; Hb may drop to <8 g/dL in severe cases
  • Pre-operative anaemia increases surgical risk — GnRH agonists or tranexamic acid used to restore Hb before surgery
  • Acute haemorrhage — rare but life-threatening; may require emergency intervention
  • Venous thromboembolism (VTE) — large fibroids causing pelvic venous stasis (rare)
🤰
Obstetric
  • Preterm labour and delivery (especially submucosal)
  • Malpresentation — large fibroids distort uterine shape, preventing cephalic presentation
  • Placenta praevia — fibroid occupies lower segment
  • Placental abruption (fibroid behind placenta)
  • Uterine rupture (rare, posterior fibroid weakening)
  • Post-partum haemorrhage (PPH) — impaired uterine contractility
  • Increased caesarean section rate (Caesarean myomectomy now often performed in select cases — FOGSI)
  • First trimester miscarriage ↑ risk (submucous)
⚡
Acute / Structural
  • Red/Carneous degeneration — acute infarction in pregnancy; severe localised pain, low-grade fever, leucocytosis; self-limiting (managed conservatively)
  • Torsion — pedunculated fibroid on stalk twists; acute abdomen; surgical emergency
  • Urinary retention — large cervical fibroid compresses urethra
  • Hydronephrosis — lateral compression of ureter (rare, chronic)
  • Prolapse of pedunculated submucosal fibroid through os — visualised on speculum
🔬
Infertility & IVF
  • Submucosal fibroids significantly ↓ IVF success rates (RR ~0.3 for live birth)
  • Even FIGO Type 3 (contacts but doesn't distort endometrium) may impair implantation
  • Intramural fibroids ≥4–5 cm — may reduce IVF success (controversial, guideline-dependent)
  • Myomectomy before IVF for symptomatic submucosal fibroids is recommended
  • Recurrent implantation failure — consider hysteroscopy to exclude cavity pathology
⚙️
Surgical Complications
  • Myomectomy → risk of conversion to hysterectomy (0.1–1%)
  • Uterine rupture in subsequent pregnancy after myomectomy (especially open)
  • UAE → post-embolisation syndrome (fever, pain, malaise); amenorrhoea risk (1–2%)
  • Morcellation concerns — if occult LMS: risk of dissemination (FDA black box warning 2014)
🔴
Malignancy Question
  • Leiomyosarcoma (LMS) does NOT arise from malignant transformation of fibroids — this is a high-yield exam fact
  • LMS arises de novo from myometrium; distinct molecular signature
  • Prevalence: ~1 in 350–500 women undergoing surgery for presumed fibroids
  • Red flags for LMS: rapid growth, post-menopausal new fibroid, irregular margins on MRI, heterogeneous signal
  • No reliable pre-operative method to exclude LMS — hence morcellation controversy
⚠️ High-Yield Exam Pearl — Red Degeneration click to expand
⚠️ High-Yield Exam Pearl

Red/Carneous degeneration is unique to pregnancy. The fibroid undergoes acute haemorrhagic infarction as its blood supply cannot keep pace with growth stimulated by pregnancy hormones. Clinical features: sudden-onset localised uterine tenderness, low-grade fever, leucocytosis, raised CRP — mimics appendicitis. Management is conservative: analgesia (paracetamol, opioids), hydration, NSAIDs (avoid after 28 weeks). It is self-limiting and does NOT require surgery.

07 — Differential Diagnosis

Differentials to Exclude

An enlarged, irregular uterus with AUB has a broad differential. Clinical examination, imaging, and sometimes hysteroscopy or endometrial biopsy are required to differentiate.

08 — Diagnosis

Diagnostic Workflow

A structured approach moving from history and examination through imaging to endoscopy and tissue sampling where indicated. The diagnosis is often made on USS; MRI is reserved for specific indications.

Diagnostic Steps 1–8 — History → USS → MRI → Biopsy click to expand
1

History

Characterise bleeding pattern (duration, frequency, amount), pain, pressure symptoms, reproductive history, contraception, medications, family history of fibroids or hereditary leiomyomatosis.

PALM-COEIN AUB classification: Document whether AUB is heavy menstrual bleeding (HMB), intermenstrual (IMB), post-coital (PCB), or post-menopausal (PMB). PALM = Polyp, Adenomyosis, Leiomyoma, Malignancy/hyperplasia. COEIN = Coagulopathy, Ovulatory dysfunction, Endometrial, Iatrogenic, Not yet classified.
2

Physical Examination

Abdominal palpation for suprapubic mass; bimanual examination; speculum examination.

Classic findings: enlarged, irregularly shaped, firm, non-tender, mobile uterus. Speculum: check cervix, look for prolapsed fibroid. Assess for anaemia signs.
3

Blood Tests

Baseline investigations to assess impact and exclude differentials.

FBC (anaemia) Ferritin βhCG (exclude pregnancy) TFTs (thyroid AUB) Coagulation screen (if HMB from adolescence → exclude vWD) LFTs + renal function (pre-operative)
4

Pelvic Ultrasound — First-Line Imaging

Transvaginal ultrasound (TVUSS) is the investigation of choice. Superior resolution for fibroid characterisation, especially submucosal involvement.

USS findings: Hypoechoic or heterogeneous whorled mass within myometrium; may have acoustic shadowing. Document: number, size, location (FIGO type where possible), relationship to endometrium and serosa, vascularity on Doppler. Transabdominal (TAUS) useful for large fibroids extending above pubic symphysis.
5

Saline Infusion Sonography (SIS) / Sonohysterography

Instillation of saline into cavity via transcervical catheter during USS. First-line for evaluation of endometrial cavity and submucosal fibroid characterisation.

Superior to TVUSS alone for submucosal fibroids. Allows FIGO subtype classification (Types 0–2) for hysteroscopic planning. Also evaluates for endometrial polyps simultaneously.
6

MRI Pelvis — Specific Indications

Not routine, but gold standard when complex anatomy, malignancy suspicion, or pre-procedural planning requires it.

Pre-UAE mapping Pre-HIFU/RFA planning Atypical USS features (LMS exclusion) Multiple fibroids (operative planning) Indeterminate pelvic mass Evaluate junctional zone (adenomyosis)
MRI fibroid signal: Low signal intensity on T2 (dense smooth muscle + collagen). Fibroids with high T2 signal or restricted diffusion raise concern for leiomyosarcoma. Gadolinium contrast: fibroids enhance variably; degenerated fibroids may not enhance.
7

Hysteroscopy

Direct endoscopic visualisation of the endometrial cavity. Diagnostic and therapeutic. Gold standard for submucosal fibroid assessment.

Indicated when: endometrial cavity pathology suspected; SIS inconclusive; pre-operative assessment of FIGO Types 0–2 for resectability; failed medical management with suspected cavity pathology. Can be combined with endometrial biopsy.
8

Endometrial Biopsy

To exclude endometrial hyperplasia or carcinoma. Not routinely required for fibroid diagnosis itself.

Indications for endometrial biopsy in fibroid patients: Age ≥45 with AUB, or any age with risk factors for endometrial cancer (obesity, DM, PCOS, tamoxifen, Lynch syndrome, treatment-resistant AUB). Post-menopausal bleeding mandates biopsy regardless.

🇮🇳 Indian Diagnostic Context:

  • • Transabdominal USS often first-line due to patient acceptability and wider equipment availability
  • • SIS availability limited to tertiary/teaching hospitals
  • • MRI access: readily available in metro cities; may require referral from district level
  • • Office hysteroscopy growing but not universally available — outpatient setting preferred where possible

09 — Management

Management Algorithm

Management is individualised based on symptom severity, fibroid characteristics (size, location, number), reproductive wishes, age, patient preference, and available resources. A stepwise approach is recommended.

Treatment Pathways — Expectant / Medical / Interventional / Surgical click to expand

Expectant

  • Asymptomatic or mildly symptomatic
  • Annual pelvic USS surveillance
  • Patient education: symptom monitoring
  • Iron supplementation if borderline anaemia
  • Reassurance re: malignant transformation myth
  • Natural regression expected post-menopause

Medical

  • Tranexamic acid — first-line HMB
  • NSAIDs — dysmenorrhoea + modest AUB
  • OCP / Progestogen — AUB control
  • LNG-IUS — if cavity not distorted
  • GnRH agonists — pre-op, 3–6 months
  • GnRH antagonists (relugolix) — oral, newer
  • Iron ± EPO — pre-op anaemia correction

Interventional

  • UAE — uterine artery embolisation
  • MRgFUS — MRI-guided focused ultrasound
  • Radiofrequency ablation (Acessa/Sonata)
  • Hysteroscopic myomectomy (Types 0–2)
  • Endometrial ablation (+ fibroid — selected)

Surgical

  • Hysteroscopic myomectomy
  • Laparoscopic myomectomy
  • Abdominal (open) myomectomy
  • Laparoscopic hysterectomy
  • Abdominal/vaginal hysterectomy
  • Note: FDA 2014 power morcellator warning

Pre-op Downsizing
hover / tap for details →
Mechanism & Use
  • Continuous GnRH stimulation → pituitary desensitisation → ↓ LH/FSH → hypoestrogenic state
  • Initial "flare" in first 2 weeks (↑ gonadotrophins) — use short-term progestogen cover
  • Reduces fibroid volume ~35–60% over 3 months
  • Pre-operative use: improve anaemia, reduce surgical blood loss, facilitate laparoscopic/vaginal approach
  • Max duration: 3–6 months — bone density loss beyond this
  • Add-back HRT (low-dose E2 + progestogen) if >3 months to protect bone
  • Fibroids regrow on stopping (rebound within 3–6 months)
⚠️ Hypoestrogenic side effects: hot flushes, bone loss (reversible), vaginal dryness, mood changes. NOT for long-term use. NOT curative.

Surgical & Interventional Options

Procedural management spans minimally-invasive interventional radiology through endoscopy to major abdominal surgery. Choice depends on fibroid characteristics, fertility goals, patient fitness, and available expertise.

Interventional Radiology
Uterine Artery Embolisation (UAE)
Bilateral embolisation of uterine arteries via femoral or radial artery approach. Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) or gelatin sponge particles. Fibroid infarction → volume reduction 40–60%. NICE NG88: recommended alternative to surgery for symptomatic fibroids. Post-embolisation syndrome: fever, pain, leucocytosis (3–5 days). Success rate: 80–90% symptom improvement. Not recommended if planning pregnancy (relative CI).

Energy-based / Ablation
Radiofrequency Ablation & MRgFUS
Acessa (laparoscopic RFA): USS-guided RFA probe placed into fibroid; thermal coagulation. Day case. Fibroid volume reduction ~40–50% at 12 months.
Sonata (transcervical RFA): Intrauterine sonication — for submucosal and intramural. No abdominal incision.
MRgFUS / ExAblate: MRI-guided focused ultrasound — non-invasive, thermal ablation. Precise targeting. Not widely available. Fibroids must have specific MRI characteristics (low T2 signal).
Uterus Preserved
Hysterectomy — Definitive Treatment

Routes

  • Total laparoscopic hysterectomy (TLH) — preferred where feasible
  • Vaginal hysterectomy — if uterus small/mobile; no bowel/bladder involvement
  • Abdominal (TAH) — large uteri, complex adhesions

Indications

  • Completed family, failed/declined all alternatives
  • Uterus too large / fibroids too numerous for myomectomy
  • Emergency haemorrhage unresponsive to conservative measures

Key Points

  • Only definitive cure — no recurrence possible
  • Ovarian conservation generally recommended in premenopausal women (bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy not routine)
  • Subtotal hysterectomy (cervix retained) → less invasive, no risk of vaginal vault prolapse; requires ongoing smears
  • FDA 2014: power morcellation contraindicated in unsuspected LMS risk — contained morcellation systems preferred

Acute Heavy Haemorrhage

Emergency scenario: fibroid-related life-threatening uterine bleeding

Emergency Protocol — Steps 01–04 click to expand
01

Resuscitation (ABC)

  • Airway — maintain, O₂ 15L non-rebreathe mask
  • 2× large-bore IV access (14–16G antecubital)
  • IV crystalloid (1L 0.9% NaCl bolus)
  • Blood transfusion: O-negative if immediate, group-specific when available
  • FBC, clotting, U&E, Group & Save / Cross-match 4 units
  • Activate major haemorrhage protocol if Hb <7 or haemodynamic instability
  • Urinary catheter + hourly urine output monitoring
02

Medical Control

  • IV Tranexamic acid 1g over 10 min STAT → can repeat at 30 min if ongoing
  • High-dose progestogen: norethisterone 5mg TDS (oral if able)
  • IV/IM Oxytocin 10 units (uterotonic — increases myometrial tone)
  • Consider ergometrine / carboprost if needed
  • Foley catheter balloon tamponade — intrauterine balloon inflated in cavity (if submucosal fibroid accessible)
  • Correct coagulopathy: FFP, cryoprecipitate, platelets as per haematology guidance (massive transfusion protocol)
03

Procedural / Interventional

  • Emergency hysteroscopic myomectomy: if submucosal fibroid accessible and bleeding localised — resect / coagulate
  • UAE (Uterine Artery Embolisation): if haemodynamically stable and IR team available — highly effective; bilateral femoral approach, immediate arterial occlusion
  • Balloon uterine tamponade (e.g., Bakri balloon) — temporising measure while definitive treatment arranged
  • Uterine compression sutures (B-Lynch) — in theatre if proceeding to open surgery
04

Surgical Escalation

  • Emergency hysterectomy: last resort when all else fails; life-saving
  • Bilateral uterine artery ligation (open): temporising, fertility-sparing alternative to hysterectomy
  • Internal iliac artery ligation: rarely performed; requires vascular surgical expertise
  • Post-operative ICU admission if significant haemorrhage / coagulopathy
  • Counsel: if hysterectomy performed, document discussions and emergency nature clearly
  • Debrief patient and family post-crisis
Special Scenario — Torsion of Pedunculated Fibroid click to expand

Presents as acute abdomen — sudden-onset severe pelvic pain, nausea/vomiting, peritonism. USS: adnexal mass with absent/impaired Doppler flow in stalk. Often initially mistaken for ovarian torsion. Management: emergency laparoscopy / laparotomy → detorsion and myomectomy (or hysterectomy). If stalk necrosed, resect fibroid with stalk. No medical temporisation indicated.

13 — High-Yield

Exam Pearls

Click each pearl to reveal the full explanation. These are the highest-frequency exam topics on uterine fibroids in USMLE, MRCP/MRCOG, and medical school finals.

01
What is the single most common benign tumour of the female pelvis?
+
Uterine leiomyoma (fibroid). Affects 70% of women by age 50 globally; 24–30% in Indian women of reproductive age (JOGI). Most common cause of hysterectomy — the most performed major gynaecological surgery in India.
02
Do fibroids undergo malignant transformation into leiomyosarcoma?
+
No — this is a classic myth. Leiomyosarcoma (LMS) arises de novo from myometrium, not from pre-existing fibroids. They are molecularly distinct entities. The probability of an apparent fibroid being LMS is ~1 in 350–500. Red flags: rapid growth (especially post-menopausal), irregular MRI margins, central necrosis, restricted diffusion on DWI. No reliable pre-operative test can exclude LMS.
03
Which fibroid types are most associated with infertility and AUB?
+
Submucosal fibroids — FIGO Types 0, 1, and 2 are most strongly associated with both abnormal uterine bleeding and infertility. They distort or contact the endometrial cavity, disrupting implantation, local haemostasis, and uterine contractility. Type 0 (pedunculated intracavitary) is fully accessible hysteroscopically. Type 3 (contacts endometrium, no visual distortion) may also impair implantation — important in IVF context.
04
What happens to fibroids after menopause? What is the key exception?
+
Fibroids regress post-menopause due to the hypoestrogenic state. They often calcify ("womb stones"). Symptoms resolve. Therefore, asymptomatic or mildly symptomatic fibroids near menopause can often be managed expectantly. Exception: Women on HRT (especially oestrogen-only) may not see regression, and fibroids may continue to grow. A new or growing post-menopausal fibroid NOT on HRT should raise suspicion for LMS.
05
What is red degeneration? When does it occur and how is it managed?
+
Red (carneous) degeneration occurs in pregnancy — typically 2nd trimester. The fibroid undergoes acute haemorrhagic infarction as rapidly increasing pregnancy hormones drive growth faster than blood supply can sustain. Features: sudden-onset severe localised uterine tenderness (fibroid site), low-grade pyrexia, leucocytosis, raised CRP. USS: heterogeneous fibroid echogenicity. Management: conservative — analgesia (paracetamol, opioids if severe), IV fluids, hydration. NSAIDs may be used before 28 weeks. Self-limiting — no surgery required. Fetal outcome generally good if managed appropriately.
06
What is the status of ulipristal acetate (Esmya) for fibroids?
+
Ulipristal acetate (UPA) is currently SUSPENDED in Europe and the UK as of 2020. The EMA suspended marketing authorisation for Esmya following post-marketing reports of serious hepatotoxicity, including liver failure requiring transplantation. The risk-benefit balance was deemed unfavourable. In exams: know it as an SPRM that was formerly used for fibroid treatment but is no longer recommended due to liver toxicity. Do not recommend it in clinical scenarios.
07
What are the rules for GnRH agonist use in fibroid management?
+
Key rules: (1) Maximum 3–6 months — bone density loss beyond this is clinically significant and potentially irreversible. (2) Always prescribe add-back HRT (low-dose oestrogen ± progestogen) if using beyond 3 months — prevents hypoestrogenic side effects without negating efficacy. (3) There is an initial "flare" in the first 1–2 weeks (gonadotrophin surge before pituitary desensitisation) — cover with progestogen or barrier method initially. (4) Fibroids regrow within 3–6 months of stopping — not curative; used pre-operatively. (5) Useful to improve anaemia before surgery and reduce uterine size to enable laparoscopic/vaginal approach.
08
Is UAE safe if a woman wants to get pregnant in future?
+
UAE is a relative contraindication if future pregnancy strongly desired. Concerns include: ovarian reserve reduction (non-target embolisation of ovarian collateral vessels), uterine artery occlusion limiting placental perfusion, increased miscarriage risk, preterm birth, malpresentation, and PPH. NICE NG88 (2018) states UAE should be discussed as an option even in women who wish to preserve fertility, with full counselling about the risks. ACOG advises myomectomy as preferred fertility-preserving surgical option. There are successful pregnancies after UAE, but data is inferior to post-myomectomy outcomes.
09
What is the most common somatic mutation in uterine fibroids?
+
MED12 mutation — present in approximately 70% of uterine leiomyomas. Affects exon 2 of the Mediator complex subunit 12 gene, which regulates RNA polymerase II transcription. This is the most common somatic driver mutation. HMGA2 rearrangements are next (~20%). The rare FH (fumarate hydratase) germline mutation causes hereditary leiomyomatosis and renal cell carcinoma (HLRCC) — presents with multiple, aggressive fibroids in young women; should prompt genetic testing and renal surveillance.
10
Name the FIGO PALM-COEIN classification of AUB and where fibroids fit.
+
PALM-COEIN classifies causes of AUB: Polyp · Adenomyosis · Leiomyoma · Malignancy & Hyperplasia — structural causes | Coagulopathy · Ovulatory dysfunction · Endometrial · Iatrogenic · Not yet classified — non-structural causes. Fibroids are the "L". The fibroid can be subclassified using FIGO Types 0–8 to specify its relationship to the endometrium (critical for treatment planning). AUB-L is most clinically significant when the fibroid is submucosal (Types 0–3).
11
What are the obstetric complications of fibroids and which fibroid type causes them?
+
Fibroids in pregnancy increase risk of: miscarriage (submucosal, especially if implantation occurs over fibroid); preterm labour (uterine irritability); malpresentation (distorted cavity/lower segment fibroids); placenta praevia (lower segment fibroid); placental abruption (retroplacental fibroid); PPH (impaired uterine contraction); CS delivery; red degeneration (pregnancy-specific). Posterior and lower segment fibroids pose greatest obstetric risk. Fibroids may enlarge in first trimester then regress in third trimester.
12
When should LNG-IUS (Mirena) NOT be used for fibroid-related AUB?
+
LNG-IUS is contraindicated when the endometrial cavity is significantly distorted by submucosal fibroids. A distorted cavity makes insertion difficult or impossible, increases expulsion rate, and may prevent the device from releasing levonorgestrel effectively. A TVUSS or SIS should be performed before insertion to assess cavity. If FIGO Types 0–2 are present with significant cavity distortion, treat the fibroid first (hysteroscopic myomectomy), then insert LNG-IUS. Also contraindicated: unexplained AUB (exclude malignancy first), active PID, pregnancy.

Question 1 of 5
A 38-year-old woman presents with heavy menstrual bleeding. Pelvic USS reveals a 3.5cm fibroid that is entirely within the endometrial cavity on a stalk. What is the FIGO classification?
A FIGO Type 1
B FIGO Type 0
C FIGO Type 3
D FIGO Type 4
B — FIGO Type 0 is correct. Type 0 = pedunculated intracavitary fibroid — entirely within the endometrial cavity on a stalk, with no intramural component. This is the most favourable type for hysteroscopic myomectomy, achievable as a single-stage procedure. Type 1 = submucosal with <50% intramural extension. Type 3 = contacts but does not distort the endometrium. Type 4 = entirely intramural.
Question 2 of 5
A 42-year-old has large symptomatic fibroids and wishes to preserve her uterus but has completed her family. She is medically fit. NICE NG88 recommends which intervention as a primary alternative to hysterectomy?
A GnRH agonist therapy long-term
B Ulipristal acetate (Esmya)
C Uterine Artery Embolisation (UAE)
D Endometrial ablation
C — UAE is correct. NICE NG88 (2018) recommends UAE as an effective, recommended alternative to surgery (hysterectomy/myomectomy) for women with symptomatic fibroids. GnRH agonists are not suitable long-term (bone loss). Ulipristal acetate is suspended due to hepatotoxicity. Endometrial ablation does not treat fibroids themselves and is contraindicated if cavity is distorted.
Question 3 of 5
A 28-year-old woman at 20 weeks gestation presents with sudden-onset severe left-sided uterine pain, low-grade fever (37.8°C), and leucocytosis. A fibroid is visible on USS with heterogeneous echogenicity. Fetal heart is present. What is the most likely diagnosis and management?
A Torsion of pedunculated fibroid — emergency laparoscopy
B Red (carneous) degeneration — conservative management
C Acute appendicitis — surgical consult
D Placental abruption — immediate delivery
B — Red degeneration is correct. This is the classic presentation: pregnancy (typically 2nd trimester), localised uterine tenderness over the fibroid site, low-grade fever, leucocytosis. The fibroid undergoes haemorrhagic infarction as blood supply cannot match growth stimulated by pregnancy hormones. Management is conservative: analgesia, IV fluids, hydration. It is self-limiting. Surgery is NOT indicated. This is a high-yield differentiating point from torsion (acute abdomen, usually non-pregnant patient) and appendicitis (RIF pain, different tenderness pattern).
Question 4 of 5
Which single genetic mutation is most commonly identified in uterine leiomyomas?
A BRCA1
B HMGA2 rearrangement
C MED12 mutation (exon 2)
D TP53 mutation
C — MED12 mutation is correct, present in approximately 70% of uterine leiomyomas. Specifically affects exon 2 of the Mediator complex subunit 12 gene. HMGA2 rearrangements are the second most common (~20%). BRCA1 is associated with breast/ovarian cancer. TP53 mutations are associated with malignancy (leiomyosarcoma, not leiomyoma). The rare FH (fumarate hydratase) germline mutation causes hereditary leiomyomatosis and renal cell carcinoma (HLRCC) — aggressive fibroids in young women, requires renal surveillance.
Question 5 of 5
A post-menopausal woman (58 years old, NOT on HRT) presents with a new pelvic mass that has grown rapidly over 6 months. Ultrasound shows a large heterogeneous uterine mass with irregular margins and central areas of low echogenicity. What is the most important concern and initial investigation?
A New fibroid — annual USS surveillance
B Adenomyosis — MRI pelvis for confirmation
C Leiomyosarcoma — urgent MRI pelvis and surgical referral
D Ovarian carcinoma — CA-125 and CT chest/abdomen/pelvis
C — Leiomyosarcoma (LMS) must be urgently excluded. Red flags present: post-menopausal woman NOT on HRT (fibroids regress post-menopause), rapid growth, heterogeneous echogenicity, irregular margins suggesting malignancy. MRI pelvis is the gold standard — LMS: heterogeneous high T2 signal, central necrosis, restricted diffusion on DWI. However, no imaging reliably excludes LMS pre-operatively — surgical removal and histological assessment is ultimately required. Surgical referral to a gynaecological oncologist is appropriate. Remember: LMS does NOT arise from malignant transformation of benign fibroids.

Key Take-Home Points

Summary & Conclusions

  1. 1. Fibroids are the most common benign pelvic tumour — affecting up to 70% of women by age 50. Most are asymptomatic.
  2. 2. Oestrogen + progesterone drive growth. Fibroids regress post-menopause. Key mutations: MED12 (70%), HMGA2, FH.
  3. 3. FIGO classification (0–8) determines management. Submucosal (Type 0–2) → hysteroscopic; Intramural/Subserosal → lap/open myomectomy or UAE.
  4. 4. USS is first-line investigation. MRI is gold standard for mapping. Saline infusion sonography for submucosal assessment.
  1. 5. Medical management first-line: Tranexamic acid for acute bleeding, LNG-IUS (Mirena) for long-term, GnRH agonists pre-operatively to shrink fibroids.
  2. 6. Hysterectomy is the only cure with zero recurrence. Myomectomy preserves fertility but has 15–30% recurrence rate.
  3. 7. LMS is de novo — NOT malignant transformation of fibroids. Power morcellation contraindicated (risk of disseminating occult sarcoma).
  4. 8. Red degeneration in pregnancy — manage conservatively with analgesics and hydration. Avoid surgery unless torsion of pedunculated fibroid.
